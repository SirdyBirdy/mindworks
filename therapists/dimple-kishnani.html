What people bring

What do people most commonly come to you with?

  • Feeling stuck in the same relationship patterns
  • Anxiety that shows up as overthinking or physical tension
  • Depression that doesn't always look like sadness
  • Burnout — especially in high-performing people
  • Grief that doesn't follow a timeline
  • Navigating a career transition or identity shift

Approach

How she works

"Sessions with me tend to be exploratory — but not aimless. I'm curious about the whole person, not just the symptom."

Dimple draws primarily on psychodynamic thinking and CBT, adapting based on what's useful. She's interested in where patterns come from, but she's equally interested in what to actually do with that understanding. Sessions move between past and present — not to stay in either one, but because both matter.

She uses structured CBT tools when something practical is needed: thought records, behavioural experiments, exposure work. When the question is bigger than a technique can answer, she slows down and explores.

Modalities she uses: CBT · Psychodynamic therapy · ACT · Trauma-informed approaches

In the room

What does a session with Dimple actually feel like?

Honest, and unhurried. There's no pressure to perform insight or arrive at breakthroughs on cue. Dimple asks a lot of questions — not to assess you, but because she's genuinely trying to understand. Sessions can be quiet when that's what's needed, and direct when that's more useful.

People often say they leave feeling like they've thought about something in a way they haven't before — not because she told them what to think, but because the space made it possible.

Her story

How did she get into this work?

Dimple didn't plan to become a therapist in the way some people do. She was drawn to psychology initially because she was trying to understand people around her — and eventually, herself. The clinical training followed the curiosity, not the other way round.

She founded Mindworks in 2016 because she kept seeing what therapy could look like when done with care — and how rarely that was the default. She wanted to build a practice where the quality of the relationship was taken as seriously as the clinical framework.

Outside of sessions, she finds herself thinking about how people carry the stories they were given about themselves — and the slow, imperfect work of rewriting them. She reads widely across attachment theory, relational psychoanalysis, and the quieter corners of neuroscience. She's particularly interested in what happens in the body during difficult conversations.

Before you start

What she wishes people knew before starting therapy

"You don't need to have it figured out before you come in. You don't need the right words, or a clear problem, or a crisis. A vague sense that something isn't working is enough. That's actually where most of the useful work starts."

Also worth knowing: progress in therapy rarely looks linear. There will be sessions that feel like nothing happened — and then you'll notice something shifted three weeks later. That's not a failure of the process. That's the process.

FAQ

Common questions

Do you only work with people who have a diagnosis?
No. About half the people I work with don't have a diagnosis — and don't need one. They're dealing with something hard and want support thinking it through. That's enough.
What's the first session actually like?
We spend most of it understanding what's brought you in. I'll ask a lot of questions — not to assess you clinically, but to get a real picture of you and your life. By the end, we'll have a rough sense of how we might work together.
How long does therapy usually take?
It depends on what you're working through. Some people find significant relief in 8–12 sessions. Others find value in longer-term work. I'll be honest with you about what I think is needed — and you're always free to stop or pause.
What's the discovery call for?
It's a free 15-minute conversation — only for therapy enquiries. We use it to see if we're a good fit before you commit to a full session. No paperwork, no pressure.
Do you offer in-person sessions?
Yes — I see clients in person at our Pune clinic (Kothrud), as well as online. If you'd like to meet in person, mention that when you book and we'll arrange it.
